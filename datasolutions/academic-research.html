Global Sequencing Market Observatory​

This section uses large-scale public repository metadata (with a particular emphasis on ENA) to approximate global academic sequencing activity and expenditure trends over 2016–2025.

Scope & caveats: the objective here is trend analysis (rates of change, relative shifts, concentration), not perfect absolute spend. Cost estimates necessarily rely on approximations where key commercial parameters are not public (e.g. flow-cell utilisation, pricing packages, discounting, etc.).

What this section aims to answer:
  • How has sequencing expenditure evolved over time, and how concentrated is spend by vendor/instrument?
  • Which platforms and instrument families are gaining share?
  • How do instrument models compare in economic positioning (cost-efficiency vs. throughput scale)?

Using ENA and related public sources, I compiled sequencing metadata estimated to cover ~95% of all experiments deposited in major repositories between 2016 and 2025, corresponding to approximately ~30 million experiments, ~30 million runs, and ~3 × 10¹⁴ reads.

For each accession level (sample, run, experiment, study), the dataset captures:

  • Sequencing platform and instrument model
  • Total read and base counts
  • Library metadata (strategy, source, selection)
  • Submitting center / institute name

Expenditure Trends and Market Concentration​

Cost estimations are derived using platform and model-specific formulas, combining vendor reagent lists and (where possible) adjustments for run-throughput differences and historical pricing changes.
Unless otherwise specified, these are best interpreted as run-level reagent cost proxies rather than fully-loaded cost-of-ownership (i.e. they do not attempt to model labour, instrument depreciation, computation, or downstream analysis).

Cost-efficiency, Scale, and Read-length Positioning​

Loading chart…

 

This bubble plot summarizes economic positioning across sequencing instrument models. Each bubble represents an instrument model, positioned by its average cost per gigabase (y-axis, log scale) and total bases sequenced (x-axis). Bubble size reflects usage volume.

  • Bottom-right: high-throughput short-read systems with strong cost-efficiency at scale (e.g. NovaSeq / HiSeq X class).
  • Upper regions: legacy or low-throughput systems with higher per-Gb costs that persist due to workflow fit, installed base, and smaller-batch needs (e.g. MiSeq / older Illumina lines).
  • Distinct clusters: long-read platforms (PacBio / Oxford Nanopore) occupy separate cost–throughput regimes, trading cost-efficiency for access to complex genomic information.

In the short-read market, throughput and efficiency at scale dominate adoption, while long-read platforms are selected for differentiated capabilities including read length, structural resolution, speed, and workflow flexibility.

 

This scatterplot shows the relationship between average read length per experiment and experiment-level sequencing cost across platforms.

Short-read platforms (Illumina, MGI, ThermoFisher) dominate the low read-length region (~100–300 bp) and span a wide cost distribution, reflecting diverse use cases and budget constraints. Long-read platforms (PacBio, Oxford Nanopore) cluster at longer read lengths with higher cost variability, consistent with their use in specialised applications rather than broad sequencing.

Limitations (interpretation guidance)
  • Repository bias: public archives reflect deposition behavior and may under-represent some clinical/industrial sequencing.
  • Utilisation uncertainty: true run utilisation (flow-cell fill, multiplexing, yield variability) is often unobserved and requires approximation.
  • Commercial opacity: discounting structures and bundles are not public, so absolute € values should be treated as proxies.
  • Interpretation focus: the most robust outputs here are relative comparisons, trends, and concentration patterns.
