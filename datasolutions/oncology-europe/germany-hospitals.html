German Hospital Landscape​

Within the European Union, cancer continues to pose a significant public health burden: every minute, approximately five people are diagnosed, while more than two lose their lives to the disease. In 2022, over 2.7 million new cancer cases were estimated across EU member countries; by 2021, cancer had become the second leading cause of death, accounting for 1.15 million deaths.

The objective of this section is to contextualise where genomic sequencing demand is most likely to concentrate at the healthcare delivery level, using Germany as a case study. Germany combines a large population, broad access to advanced oncology care, and a decentralised hospital system, making it a useful reference for understanding regional variation in sequencing demand across Europe.

To support this analysis, I compiled cancer incidence data from 2020 across major tumour types including lung, colorectal, ovarian, breast, prostate, pancreatic, melanoma, leukaemia, uterine, and non-Hodgkin lymphoma. These data were then mapped to hospitals most likely to be involved in diagnosis and ongoing treatment, providing a first-order approximation of institutional concentration of oncology activity.

Below is a snapshot showing the top five hospitals by cancer-related case volume (diagnoses and ongoing treatments) in three federal states: Berlin, Bavaria, and North Rhine-Westphalia (NRW).





Clear regional differences emerge. Berlin, as a highly urbanised city-state, shows strong concentration of oncology activity within a small number of large institutions, most prominently Charité – Universitätsmedizin Berlin, Europe’s largest university hospital. In contrast, Bavaria and North Rhine-Westphalia exhibit a more distributed structure, with oncology care spread across multiple high-volume centres reflecting their polycentric urban geography.

From a market intelligence perspective, this distinction is relevant: regions with concentrated oncology activity are more likely to act as early adopters of new sequencing technologies via a small number of reference centres, whereas more distributed regions tend to adopt incrementally through regional networks and phased procurement strategies.
